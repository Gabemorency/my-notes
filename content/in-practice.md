# In Practice

The argument so far has been conceptual. Here is what it looks like when it runs.

---

**The Commons Initiative** is a small nonprofit focused on community infrastructure in mid-sized cities. Twelve staff. A board of nine. Leadership has turned over twice in three years. They have been using a version of this system for eighteen months.

Before that, their institutional knowledge lived across four Google Drives, two Slack workspaces, a shared Notion that three people had edit access to, and the personal notes of a former executive director who is no longer reachable.

They know exactly how much that cost them.

> [!note] About this case study
> The Commons Initiative is a fictional organization. The documents below are representative examples — built to show what each document type actually looks like inside a running knowledge system, not to describe a real institution.

---

## What Their Knowledge Base Contains

The system is organized around five types of documents. Each one solves a different piece of the fragmentation problem.

| Document type | What it captures | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| [[ex-meeting\|Meeting notes]] | Decisions made, who was present, what was said | Prevents "I don't remember why we did that" |
| [[ex-contacts\|Contact manager]] | Relationships, context, history with people | Survives staff turnover |
| [[ex-decisions\|Decision log]] | Major choices with full reasoning | Makes institutional logic visible |
| [[ex-project\|Project tracking]] | Status, dependencies, owners, timelines | One source of truth for active work |
| [[ex-governance\|Governing documents]] | Values, roles, decision-making process | The organization's operating constitution |

---

## How They Use It

Notes are written in Obsidian and committed to a private repository. Selected documents — including this case study — are published through Quartz to a restricted site accessible only to staff and board members.

The graph view is reviewed quarterly as part of their leadership transitions. New staff are onboarded using the decision log and governing documents before their first week ends. Contact notes are updated after every significant conversation.

Eighteen months in, they conducted their third leadership transition. It took two weeks instead of four months.
