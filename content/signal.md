# Signal

You found this page. That means you were reading carefully — or you were curious enough to click something that didn't announce itself as a link.

That act of attention is not incidental to the research. It's part of what this project is about.

---

Most knowledge management conversations are really conversations about information management. How do we store things? How do we organize them? How do we make sure they don't get lost?

Those are real questions. But they miss something important.

**Information is raw material. Knowledge is structured.** A folder full of documents is information. A system that understands how those documents relate to each other — which ones contradict each other, which ones build on each other, which ones were written by the same person in the same week and reflect the same underlying assumption — that is something closer to knowledge.

The distinction matters because the tools we build for information management often fail us as knowledge management tools. We design for retrieval — can I find the file? — when we should be designing for understanding — can I make sense of what I have?

---

## What Relational Actually Means

The word "relational" gets overused in knowledge management contexts. But it points at something real.

A note about a decision is more useful when you can see, in the same view, the notes that preceded that decision, the notes that followed from it, and the notes written by the people most affected by it. Not just because it adds context — but because context is often the actual knowledge. The raw decision, stripped of its relationships, is just data.

Obsidian's bidirectional links are one implementation of this principle. The Quartz graph view makes it visible. But the underlying idea is older and more general: knowledge lives in [relationships](the-why), not in nodes.
