# Why This

Most research projects start with a question. This one started with a frustration.

I kept watching organizations — real ones, with real people doing real work — lose things they couldn't afford to lose. Not data. Not files. *Understanding*. The institutional knowledge of how decisions got made, why things work the way they do, what was tried before and why it didn't work.

The information was usually there somewhere. It just wasn't organized in a way that let anyone get to it. Or it was organized in someone's head, and that person had left. Or it was in a tool that no one used anymore, behind a login no one had.

---

## The Tool Problem

Every time I tried to point at a solution, I ran into the same wall: the tools that actually solve this problem are enterprise tools with enterprise prices. And the organizations that most need continuity — startups, nonprofits, new institutions, small teams — are exactly the organizations that can't afford them.

So they use what they have. Google Drive. Slack. Notion. Email. Tools that are good at what they're designed for, and not designed for this.

The question that started this research was simple: **what would it take to build something that actually works, for free, for everyone?**

---

## Why Markdown. Why Obsidian. Why This Stack.

The answer I kept coming back to was: local-first, open-format, version-controlled, publishable.

Local-first because you should own your knowledge. Open-format because knowledge you can't read in ten years isn't really knowledge you kept. Version-controlled because traceability is a form of memory. Publishable because knowledge that can't be shared has limited reach.

Obsidian + Quartz + GitHub Pages is not a perfect answer. But it's a coherent one. And it's available to anyone with a computer and a GitHub account.

---

## What I'm Building Toward

The goal isn't a personal notes website. The goal is a proof of concept for a knowledge architecture — one that can be documented, replicated, and adapted by small organizations that currently have no good options.

If the research holds, this stack becomes a template. A starting point. A way for a twelve-person nonprofit or a new research lab to build something that will actually preserve what they know.

That's worth figuring out.
