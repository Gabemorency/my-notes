---

## What This Project Is Really About

Most of what gets written about knowledge management is about tools. Which app to use. How to set up your folders. What plugins to install. This project tries to ask a different question: what does it mean for an institution to *know* something — and what would it take to build infrastructure that preserves that?

The answer I keep coming back to is uncomfortable: most organizations don't actually know things. Individuals within them know things. The organization just has files.

The gap between those two states is what this research is trying to close.

---

## The Honest Version of the Hypothesis

The polished version of the research says: a local-first, markdown-based, version-controlled knowledge architecture can serve as an affordable alternative to enterprise knowledge management systems for small organizations.

The honest version says: I think most small organizations are one leadership transition away from losing a significant chunk of what makes them work, and no one has built them a real tool to prevent that, because the market for "free and local-first" is hard to monetize.

This is a research project about that gap.

---

## Why It's Built This Way

The site you're exploring is itself the prototype. The hidden pages, the buried links, the graph view, the plain markdown files — none of that is decoration. It's an argument about what a knowledge system should feel like to navigate.

Information that rewards attention is more likely to be used. Systems that feel alive are more likely to be maintained. Structure that carries meaning survives transitions better than structure that just holds files.

If you made it here, you already understand that better than most.
